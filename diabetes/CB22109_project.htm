Background

 

The rise of chronic non-communicable diseases represents a significant and growing challenge to global public health. Among these, diabetes has emerged as one of the most prevalent and burdensome conditions, affecting millions of individuals worldwide. The World Health Organization has repeatedly emphasized the urgent need for early detection and prevention strategies to combat the increasing incidence of diabetes, particularly in rapidly urbanizing societies.

 

In urban environmentswhere sedentary lifestyles, unhealthy diets, and limited physical activity are commonthe prevalence of diabetes is accelerating at an alarming rate. These lifestyle patterns, coupled with environmental and genetic risk factors, have created a scenario in which late diagnosis is frequent, often leading to severe complications such as cardiovascular disease, kidney failure, and neuropathy. Such outcomes not only compromise patients' quality of life but also place immense strain on healthcare systems in terms of both cost and capacity.

 

Within the broader framework of smart cities, there is a clear opportunity to leverage technology to improve public health outcomes. Smart cities emphasize the use of data-driven technologies, such as Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML), to enhance the efficiency and responsiveness of urban services. In this context, predictive healthcare systems powered by machine learning can serve as early warning tools, enabling proactive management of chronic diseases like diabetes.

 

By analyzing clinical and lifestyle data, machine learning models can identify high-risk individuals before symptoms become severe or irreversible. Such models can be integrated into mobile health applications, wearable devices, or community healthcare portals, thereby providing accessible and timely alerts to both patients and healthcare providers. This aligns closely with the vision of "Smart Healthcare," where digital innovation is used to promote preventive care, optimize resource utilization, and ultimately improve population health outcomes.

 

The early prediction of diabetes through machine learning not only facilitates timely intervention but also supports a shift from reactive to preventive healthcare. This transformation holds significant value in reducing the burden on hospitals, enhancing the allocation of medical resources, and improving patient engagement in self-care. Therefore, the development of a machine learning application for early diabetes prediction is not only timely and relevant but also aligns with national and global goals for sustainable urban health systems.

Machine Learning Modelling

 

Proposed Machine Learning Models: Logistic Regression and Random Forest

 

In the context of developing a machine learning model for early diabetes detection, two well-established algorithms have been selected: Logistic Regression and Random Forest Classifier. Both are widely applied in the healthcare domain and are particularly suitable for binary classification tasks, which aligns with the objective of predicting whether a patient is diabetic or not based on clinical and demographic data.

 

1.Logistic Regression

 


Logistic Regression is a supervised learning algorithm used for binary classification problems. It models the probability of a binary outcome (e.g., diabetic vs. non-diabetic) by applying a logistic (sigmoid) function to a linear combination of input features. The output is a value between 0 and 1, representing the likelihood of a data point belonging to the positive class. A threshold value (commonly 0.5) is then applied to assign class labels.

Justification
Logistic Regression is chosen as a baseline model due to its simplicity, interpretability, and efficiency. It provides clear and quantifiable insight into how each independent variable affects the probability of the target outcome. In medical applications, such interpretability is especially important, as it allows clinicians to understand and validate the reasoning behind model predictions. For example, a positive coefficient for HbA1c_level would indicate that higher values of this feature increase the likelihood of diabetes, which aligns with medical knowledge.

 

Moreover, Logistic Regression is computationally efficient, making it suitable for large datasets such as the one used in this project, which consists of over 100,000 records. It can also be extended with regularization techniques to prevent overfitting. In cases where the dataset is imbalanced, as is often the case in disease prediction tasks, Logistic Regression supports class weighting strategies to help balance sensitivity and specificity.

 

Overall, Logistic Regression serves as an effective and interpretable starting point for predictive modeling in healthcare, offering both practical utility and a strong statistical foundation.

2. Random Forest Classifier

Description
Random Forest is an ensemble-based learning algorithm that builds a collection of decision trees during training time and outputs the mode of the classes (for classification) or mean prediction (for regression) of the individual trees. It operates by creating multiple decision trees using bootstrapped samples from the training data and introducing feature randomness when splitting nodes. This process reduces variance and improves model generalization.

Justification


Random Forest is selected for its ability to model complex, non-linear relationships between features and the target variable, which is often necessary in medical datasets where patient conditions may not follow linear trends. Unlike Logistic Regression, which assumes linear separability, Random Forest does not require any assumptions about data distribution or feature relationships. It is capable of capturing interactions between variables such as age, BMI, glucose levels, and comorbid conditions like hypertension and heart disease.

 

One of the key strengths of Random Forest is its robustness. It performs well in the presence of noisy data, missing values, and outliers. Additionally, it provides feature importance scores that can help identify the most influential variables contributing to diabetes prediction, aiding both in model interpretation and potential clinical decision-making.

Random Forest is also well-suited for imbalanced datasets, which is critical in this case, as preliminary analysis of the dataset revealed that the proportion of diabetic patients is significantly lower than that of non-diabetic patients.

\

 

Summary and Comparative Justification

Both Logistic Regression and Random Forest are suitable algorithms for the task of diabetes prediction, but they offer different strengths. Logistic Regression is highly interpretable, fast, and statistically grounded, making it ideal for establishing a transparent baseline model. It allows stakeholders such as medical professionals and decision-makers to clearly understand how the model reaches its conclusions.

Random Forest, in contrast, provides greater predictive power, especially in capturing complex, non-linear relationships and interactions between variables. It is less prone to overfitting than a single decision tree and is effective in handling data irregularities such as missing values and noise. While it is more complex and less interpretable than Logistic Regression, the trade-off is justified by its high classification performance and robustness.

By applying both models in the development process, we can compare their results and select the one that offers the best balance between accuracy, interpretability, and suitability for deployment in a real-world healthcare application. The use of both models also ensures that the machine learning pipeline remains flexible, comprehensive, and well-aligned with the objectives of smart healthcare systems.

 

Development Proses

 

1. Evaluation of model performance across all folds.

 

In this project, Stratified K-Fold cross-validation was used instead of standard K-Fold because the diabetes dataset is highly imbalanced, with significantly more non-diabetic (No) cases compared to diabetic (Yes) cases. Using regular K-Fold could result in folds where the distribution of the target variable is not representative of the overall dataset. For example, some folds might contain very few or no diabetic cases at all, which would negatively impact the training and evaluation of the model.

Stratified K-Fold addresses this issue by ensuring that each fold maintains the same proportion of diabetic and non-diabetic cases as in the full dataset. This helps provide a fair and consistent evaluation of model performance across all folds. It also ensures that the model is trained and tested on data that reflects the real-world class imbalance, leading to more reliable and unbiased accuracy scores.

The Random Forest model achieves a better balance between correctly identifying positive cases and avoiding incorrect classifications, making it the more robust choice for this application.

    I.  What is Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE)?

Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE) is a wrapper-based feature selection method used to identify and retain only the most important features for a predictive model. The main idea behind RFE is to recursively remove features that contribute the least to the predictive power of the model, based on some importance metric, and then build the model again with the remaining features.

 

 

   II.  2. Why use RFE with Logistic Regression?

Logistic Regression is a linear model that outputs the probability of a binary classification. Each input feature in logistic regression has a corresponding coefficient (also called a weight). These coefficients represent the influence of the feature on the models prediction. A higher absolute value of a coefficient indicates that the feature is more influential.

RFE leverages these coefficients to determine which features are less important and should be removed. Using RFE with logistic regression helps in simplifying the model, reducing overfitting, and improving interpretability.

Mean Decrease in Impurity (MDI) is used for feature selection in the Random Forest algorithm 

 

The Mean Decrease in Impurity (MDI) method is selected for feature selection in the Random Forest algorithm because it is efficient, model-driven, and directly aligned with the internal mechanism of decision trees. This method evaluates the importance of each feature based on how much it helps reduce the impurity when making splits during training.

Unlike generic filter methods that rely solely on statistical measures, MDI provides a more accurate reflection of each features actual contribution to the models decision-making process. It also handles complex interactions and non-linear relationships naturally, which is a core strength of Random Forest. In addition, MDI is automatically calculated during model training, which avoids the need for separate feature selection steps, saving both time and computational resources.

Comprehensive analysis

 

In this project, two machine learning algorithmsLogistic Regression and Random Forest Classifierwere developed and evaluated for the purpose of predicting diabetes using a large clinical dataset. Both models were carefully selected based on their suitability for binary classification tasks and their established performance in healthcare-related applications. A detailed comparative analysis was conducted based on performance metrics, feature selection strategies, interpretability, and deployment considerations. This section presents a thorough evaluation of both models and provides a well-justified recommendation for deployment.

Logistic Regression is a linear, probabilistic model that estimates the likelihood of a binary outcome based on a set of input features. It is widely used in healthcare due to its simplicity and interpretability. In this study, Logistic Regression achieved an overall accuracy of 96%, a precision of 0.87, a recall of 0.64, and an F1-score of 0.74. These metrics indicate that the model is generally effective at distinguishing between diabetic and non-diabetic individuals. However, the recall value suggests that it missed a significant number of actual positive cases. This shortcoming is critical in the context of healthcare, where failing to identify a diabetic patient can lead to serious consequences.

Feature selection for Logistic Regression was performed using Recursive Feature Elimination (RFE), a technique that ranks features based on their contribution to the predictive power of the model. The top selected features included HbA1c_level, heart_disease, gender, and hypertension. These features are clinically relevant and contribute to the models interpretability, which is one of the primary strengths of Logistic Regression. The ability to understand how each variable affects the outcome is crucial for medical professionals who require transparent and explainable models to support clinical decisions.

On the other hand, the Random Forest Classifier is a powerful ensemble learning algorithm that constructs multiple decision trees and aggregates their results to improve predictive performance. Unlike Logistic Regression, it does not assume any linear relationship among features and is capable of capturing complex, non-linear interactions between variables. In this project, the Random Forest model outperformed Logistic Regression across all evaluated metrics. It achieved an accuracy of 97%, a precision of 0.93, a recall of 0.69, and an F1-score of 0.79. These results demonstrate that the model was more effective at identifying diabetic cases, with fewer false positives and false negatives.

Feature importance in the Random Forest model was determined using the Mean Decrease in Impurity (MDI), a method that quantifies how much each feature contributes to reducing uncertainty during the decision tree splits. The most influential features were HbA1c_level, blood_glucose_level, bmi, and age. These features are clinically significant indicators of diabetes and their high importance scores reinforce the reliability of the models predictions. Moreover, the Random Forest model demonstrated strong robustness in handling the imbalanced dataset, where only about 8.5% of the samples were diabetic cases. Its ability to maintain high recall in such conditions is a notable advantage, particularly in medical diagnostics where the cost of missing a positive case is high.

From a deployment perspective, Random Forest offers a compelling combination of predictive accuracy and reliability. Although it is more complex and less interpretable than Logistic Regression, its improved performance justifies the trade-off in interpretability, especially in applications where predictive accuracy is of paramount importance. Additionally, modern deployment tools and model explanation frameworks (such as SHAP or LIME) can be integrated to enhance the interpretability of Random Forest models, making them more acceptable in clinical environments.

In conclusion, while Logistic Regression provides a transparent and interpretable approach, its relatively lower recall and F1-score make it less suitable for deployment in a healthcare setting where early and accurate detection of diabetes is essential. The Random Forest Classifier, with its superior performance across all critical evaluation metrics, stronger handling of imbalanced data, and ability to model complex relationships, emerges as the most appropriate model for deployment. It provides a balanced trade-off between predictive power and operational feasibility, making it a more effective and reliable solution for real-world diabetes prediction in support of smart healthcare initiatives
